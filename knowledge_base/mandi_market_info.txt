MANDI PRICES, MARKET INTELLIGENCE, AND AGRI-TRADE
===================================================

## UNDERSTANDING MANDI PRICE SYSTEM IN INDIA

INDIA'S AGRICULTURAL MARKETING STRUCTURE:
- APMC (Agricultural Produce Market Committee) Mandis: State-regulated wholesale markets
- Total: 7,000+ principal markets and 22,000+ sub/periodic markets
- Daily arrivals and price data collected by AGMARKNET (agmarknet.gov.in)
- e-NAM (National Agriculture Market): Online platform integrating APMCs

MANDI PRICE COMPONENTS:
- Modal Price: Most common transaction price (used for MSP comparison)
- Minimum Price: Lowest transacted price
- Maximum Price: Highest transacted price
- Arrival Volume: Total quantity arriving in mandi (quintals)

FACTORS AFFECTING MANDI PRICES:
1. Seasonal supply: prices lowest at harvest; highest in lean season
2. MSP: Government support price; market should not go below MSP
3. Export demand: Higher export = higher domestic prices
4. Import policy: Lower import duty = lower domestic prices
5. Weather: Poor crop = higher prices; bumper crop = price crash
6. Processing industry demand: Particularly for cotton, sugarcane, oilseeds
7. Procurement by agencies: Government buying at MSP supports floor price

## PRICE TRENDS FOR MAJOR COMMODITIES

WHEAT:
- Harvest season price (March–April): Near MSP (₹2,275/q in 2024–25)
- Off-season price (November–December): ₹2,400–2,600/q
- Post-harvest storage gain: ₹100–200/q per month
- Major mandis: Khanna (Punjab), Hapur (UP), Sriganganagar (Raj)

RICE:
- Paddy (Common) harvest price: ₹2,200–2,400/q
- Basmati (1121): ₹4,500–6,500/q depending on quality
- Major mandis: Karnal (Haryana), Fazilka (Punjab), Dhampur (UP)

SOYBEAN:
- Harvest (October–November): Near MSP ₹4,892/q
- Pre-sowing demand (May–June): ₹5,200–5,500/q
- Oil mill purchase price varies with soy oil international price

COTTON:
- Kapas (seed cotton): MSP + ₹200–500/q premium for quality cotton
- CCI procurement at MSP if market falls below
- Major mandis: Rajkot (Gujarat), Adilabad (Telangana), Guntur (AP)

ONION:
- Most volatile crop; price range: ₹500–7,000/q in same year
- Peak price: October–December (kharif marketing)
- Low price: March–May (rabi harvest)
- Major mandis: Lasalgaon (Maharashtra) – Asia's largest onion market
- Export destination: Bangladesh, Malaysia, Sri Lanka, UAE

TOMATO:
- Extreme price volatility: ₹200 to ₹8,000/q in same year
- Price spikes during unseasonal rain or transportation disruption
- Major surplus: Karnataka (Kolar), AP (Madanapalle), Maharashtra (Narayangaon)

POTATO:
- Cold storage plays key role in price regulation
- Harvest price (March–April): ₹400–700/q
- May–August (storage period): ₹600–1000/q
- Off-storage (October onwards): ₹500–800/q
- Major area: UP (Agra, Kanpur), Punjab, West Bengal

PULSES (AAHAR DALS):
- Government actively procures through NAFED to stabilize prices
- Tur Dal: ₹6,000–9,000/q range over last 5 years
- Moong: ₹7,000–9,500/q
- Masur: ₹5,000–7,000/q
- Buffer stock maintained by government: 2–3 lakh tonnes per pulse

## MARKET INTELLIGENCE AND PRICE FORECASTING

SEASONAL PRICE INDEX (sample for reference):
- January = 110 (slightly above average; lean season)
- February = 115 (lean season; prices high)
- March = 100 (harvest begins; prices moderate)
- April = 90 (peak harvest; prices fall)
- May = 85 (post-harvest; lowest prices)
- June = 90 (storage stocks selling)
- July = 95 (kharif planting; moderate demand)
- August = 100 (average)
- September = 105 (pre-arrival speculation)
- October = 100 (new kharif arrives; prices stable)
- November = 95 (kharif marketing; prices soften)
- December = 105 (rabi sowing; demand rises)

MARKET TIMING STRATEGY:
- Do NOT sell immediately at harvest; prices are lowest
- Store if storage available; sell in lean season (Feb–May for kharif crops)
- Forward selling/contract farming reduces price risk
- Join FPO (Farmer Producer Organization) for collective bargaining

## FPO (FARMER PRODUCER ORGANIZATIONS)

WHAT IS FPO:
- A legally registered organization (usually cooperative) of farmers
- Can collectively buy inputs, sell produce, process, and add value
- Eligible for government support under SFAC, NABARD, RKVY

BENEFITS:
- Bulk purchasing of inputs at lower price (10–20% savings)
- Collective selling at better prices (5–15% higher)
- Access to formal credit (as FPO, not individual)
- Cold storage, processing infrastructure support
- Direct export opportunities

GOVERNMENT SUPPORT:
- 10,000 FPO formation target by 2027–28 (GOI scheme)
- ₹18 lakh equity grant + ₹15 lakh credit guarantee per FPO
- Professional management support for 3 years

HOW TO JOIN/FORM:
- Minimum 11 farmers required for registration
- Register under Companies Act 2013 (Section 8) or State Co-op Act
- Contact: NABARD district office, SFAC, or state agriculture department

## POST-HARVEST MANAGEMENT

STORAGE TECHNOLOGIES:
1. On-farm storage bags (HDPE/PP):
   - Zero energy cool chamber: 20×20×20 feet; 25–30°C temp; keeps vegetables fresh
   - PUSA zero energy chamber: Earthen pots, wet sand; no electricity
   
2. Metal Bins (Pusa Bin):
   - 1.5–2 quintal capacity; prevents rat/insect damage
   - Cost: ₹2,000–4,000/unit
   - Good for pulses, wheat, gram storage for 6–8 months

3. HDPE Woven Sacks with hermetic technology:
   - IRRI bags (SuperGrain bags): eliminate oxygen; prevent insect pests
   - Cost: ₹150–300/bag (50 kg); reusable for 5 years
   - No pesticide required!

4. Cold Storage:
   - For potato: 2–4°C; 90–95% RH; 8–10 months storage life
   - For apple/temperate fruits: 0–1°C; CA storage (Controlled Atmosphere) preferred
   - For vegetables/flowers: 8–12°C; pre-cooling within 6 hours of harvest important
   - Subsidy: 35% for general; 50% for NE/hilly states under NHB scheme

POST-HARVEST LOSSES:
- India loses 30–35% of food production post-harvest (₹90,000 crore/year)
- Highest losses: Fruits (30–40%), Vegetables (25–35%), Pulses (8–10%)
- Major causes: Improper storage, pest infestation, transport damage, market glut

COLD CHAIN DEVELOPMENT:
- NHB (National Horticulture Board): subsidy for pack houses, cold storage
- MoFPI (Ministry of Food Processing): subsidy for food processing units, cold chain
- APEDA: Export support; helps with cold chain infrastructure for exports

## EXPORT OPPORTUNITIES FOR INDIAN FARMERS

HIGH-VALUE EXPORT CROPS:
1. Basmati Rice: ₹30,000–50,000 crore annual exports; main buyers: Iran, Saudi Arabia, UAE
2. Grapes: Nashik grapes exported to Europe; ₹3,000–4,000/kg in EU
3. Pomegranate: ₹3,000+ crore exports; buyers: Bangladesh, UAE, UK
4. Mango: Alphonso/Kesar to UK, USA, Japan; ₹1,500–3,000 crore
5. Spices: India #1 exporter; pepper, turmeric, chili, cumin
6. Cotton: Raw cotton + yarn exports to Bangladesh, Vietnam, China

HOW FARMERS CAN ACCESS EXPORT:
- Join APEDA-registered exporter as supplier
- Get GlobalGAP certification for EU markets (support available through APEDA)
- Organic certification for premium prices
- Join FPO that is linked to exporters
